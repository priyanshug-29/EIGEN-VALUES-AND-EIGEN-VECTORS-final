UNIT 1 · LINEAR ALGEBRA

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

A complete, simple-language guide — clear definitions, matrix visuals, worked steps, and real-world applications.

📘 Introduction

For a square matrix A, an eigenvector is a special non-zero vector whose direction does not change when A acts on it — it is only stretched or shrunk. The amount of stretching is called the eigenvalue.

📖 Definitions (Textbook)

DEF 1Eigenvalue

Let A be an n×n square matrix. A scalar λ is called an eigenvalue (or characteristic value) of A if there exists a non-zero vector v such that

A v = λ v

DEF 2Eigenvector

The non-zero vector v satisfying Av = λv is called an eigenvector (or characteristic vector) of A corresponding to the eigenvalue λ.

DEF 3Characteristic Equation

The equation |A − λI| = 0 is called the characteristic equation of A. Its roots give the eigenvalues.

Characteristic Equation

For a 2×2 matrix it expands to:

λ² − (trace A) λ + det(A) = 0

For a 3×3 matrix:

λ³ − S₁ λ² + S₂ λ − S₃ = 0

where S₁ = trace(A), S₂ = sum of 2×2 principal minors, S₃ = det(A).

📐 Properties & Theorems

P1 Sum of eigenvalues = trace(A).

P2 Product of eigenvalues = det(A).

P3 Eigenvalues of Aᵀ = Eigenvalues of A.

P4 Eigenvalues of A⁻¹ are 1/λᵢ.

P5 Eigenvalues of Aᵏ are λᵢᵏ.

P6 A triangular matrix has its diagonal entries as eigenvalues.

Key Formulas

|A − λI| = 0
Σ λᵢ = trace(A)   |   Π λᵢ = det(A)

⭐ Why Eigenvalues are Important

The Big Idea

Eigenvalues reveal the hidden structure of a matrix — its natural directions, stability, energy, and dominant behavior. Almost every modern technology that handles data, motion, or vibration uses them.

  • Compress huge data while keeping the most information
  • Predict whether a system is stable or will collapse
  • Rank web pages, recommend movies, recognize faces
  • Solve quantum-mechanics & vibration problems
